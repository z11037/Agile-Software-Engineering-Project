## I. Teamwork and agile practice in class 

1. Product goal is clear. The team can explain the purpose of the app in simple words. 
2. Users and client are understood. The team keeps the needs of new English learners in mind. 
3. The backlog is visible and well kept. Tasks, stories, or features are easy to see. 
4. The backlog is ordered by value. The team works on the most important things first. 
5. User stories or requirements are clear. The work items are specific enough to build and test. 
6. The sprint goal is clear. The team knows what this cycle is supposed to achieve. 
7. Sprint planning and task breakdown are realistic. The team does not promise more than it can 
finish. 
8. Class time is used well. The team stays on task and works at a steady pace. 
9. Progress is transparent. The board or tracker is updated and shows the real status. 
10. Short team updates are useful. Students share progress, blockers, and next steps clearly. 
11. Communication is respectful and professional. Team members listen well and speak politely. 
12. Team climate is positive. Members encourage one another and keep the group working well. 
13. The team is self managing. Students take initiative instead of waiting for the teacher to decide 
everything. 
14. Workload is shared fairly. One or two students do not carry the whole project alone. 
15. Risks and blockers are raised early. The team does not hide problems. 
16. The team responds to feedback and change. Comments from the lecturer or client lead to 
visible improvement. 
17. Working software is delivered regularly. The team shows real progress, not only talk or slides. 
18. A shared standard for finished work is clear and followed. The team has a clear idea of what 
counts as done. 
19. Testing and technical care are visible. Bugs are checked, fixed, and discussed seriously. 
20. Reflection and improvement are visible. The team learns from each cycle and changes how it 
works. 

## II. Lecturer rubric for the presentation and software demonstration 

1. Opening is clear. The team starts by explaining what the project is. 
2. The problem is explained well. The audience understands why this app is needed. 
3. Target users are clear. The team explains who will use the app. 
4. Scope and priorities are clear. The audience understands what the project includes and what it 
does not include. 
5. The presentation structure is logical. The talk moves in a clear order. 
6. Movement between speakers is smooth. Team members change over naturally. 
7. Slides or visuals are readable and professional. Text is clear and not crowded. 
8. Speaking is clear, audible, and confident. Students speak to the audience, not only to the 
screen. 
9. Time is managed well. The group finishes within the given time. 
10. Participation is balanced. More than one student contributes meaningfully. 
11. The demo uses a realistic user scenario. The team shows the app through a believable case. 
12. The demo shows the main user journey. The audience can see the most important flow. 
13. Core features work as claimed. The demo shows real functions, not only mock screens. 
14. Evidence of testing and quality checking is shown. The team can explain how it checked the 
software. 
15. The interface is understandable during the demo. The audience can easily follow what is 
happening. 
16. Technical choices are explained clearly. The team can explain why it built the system this 
way. 
17. Agile process and evidence of work are shown clearly. The team can show backlog items, 
progress, reviews, or changes during development. 
18. The team shows what changed after feedback. Improvement from the earlier presentation is 
visible. 
19. Questions are answered accurately and honestly. Students do not guess carelessly. 
20. The conclusion explains value, limits, and next steps. The ending is clear and professional.

## III. Client rubric for the presentation and software demonstration 

1. The product fits the client brief. The app matches the need discussed with the client. 
2. The benefit for new students is clear. The client can see how the app helps them. 
3. The English level suits the target learners. The language is not too hard or too easy. 
4. English content is accurate. Grammar, spelling, and vocabulary are correct. 
5. Examples, exercises, and media support real English use. The content feels useful in real 
student life. 
6. Instructions are clear and simple. A beginner can understand what to do. 
7. First use is easy for a new learner. The app is easy to start using without confusion. 
8. The learning path is logical. Activities follow a sensible order. 
9. The app teaches before it tests when needed. Learners get enough support before being 
judged. 
10. Practice activities are meaningful. The tasks help real learning, not just clicking. 
11. Feedback after answers is helpful. Learners understand what was right, wrong, or how to 
improve. 
12. Progress tracking is useful. Learners can see growth over time. 
13. Navigation is easy. Users can move through the app without getting lost. 
14. Text is easy to read. Screen content is clear and comfortable to follow. 
15. Buttons, labels, and icons are clear. Users understand what each action means. 
16. Layout is consistent and not confusing. Similar screens behave in similar ways. 
17. The design helps users avoid mistakes. The app reduces avoidable confusion and wrong input. 
18. Help and error messages are clear. If something goes wrong, the learner knows what to do 
next. 
19. Accessibility and inclusion are considered. The app is understandable and usable for a wide 
range of learners. 
20. Students would want to use it, and the client would recommend it. The app feels useful, 
trustworthy, and worth adopting.
